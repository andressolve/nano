The View From Above

A Tale of Perspective

Rome · 170 CE

Chapter 1

The Humiliation

Gaius ran. He didn't know where he was going—just away. Away from the stage, away from the laughter, away from all those faces.

The Festival of Minerva was supposed to be his moment. He had practiced the poem for weeks. Every word, every pause, every gesture. His father had said he would make the family proud.

Instead, his mind had gone blank. He had stood there, mouth open, while the silence stretched. Then someone had laughed. Then everyone.

His sandals slapped against the stone paths of the palace gardens. The evening light was turning golden, but Gaius didn't notice. All he could hear was the echo of that laughter, playing over and over in his head.

Everyone saw. Everyone knows. I'll never live this down.

Chapter 2

The Encounter

Gaius rounded a corner and nearly collided with an older man walking alone among the cypress trees.

"Forgive me!" Gaius stumbled backward, his face flushing even deeper. "I wasn't watching where I was—"

He stopped. The man was looking at him with calm, curious eyes. He wore simple gray-brown robes, nothing fancy. His beard was gray and neatly trimmed, his face weathered but kind.

"You seem troubled, young man," the stranger said. His voice was gentle, unhurried. "What weighs on you so heavily that you must run from it?"

Gaius shook his head. "It's nothing. I just—I made a fool of myself. In front of everyone."

"Ah." The man nodded slowly. "I'm Marcus. Would you like to walk with me for a moment? Sometimes it helps to speak of these things."

Chapter 3

The Problem

They walked together along the garden path. Something about the man's quiet presence made Gaius want to talk.

"I was supposed to recite a poem at the festival," Gaius said, the words tumbling out. "I practiced for weeks. But when I got up there, I just... forgot. Everything. Everyone was watching, and I couldn't remember a single word."

He felt his eyes stinging. "They laughed at me. All of them. My father was there. My teachers. Everyone I know."

"And now?" Marcus asked.

"Now my life is ruined," Gaius said miserably. "I'll never be able to show my face again. They'll remember this forever."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. Then he asked:

"Is it? Truly ruined?"

Chapter 4

The First Shift

Gaius frowned. "What do you mean?"

"I'd like to try something with you," Marcus said. "A way of seeing that I practice when I feel overwhelmed. Would you humor an old man?"

Gaius shrugged. What did he have to lose?

"Close your eyes," Marcus said. "Imagine you are a bird—an eagle, perhaps—rising up from this very spot. You lift above these gardens. What do you see?"

Gaius closed his eyes and tried to picture it. "I see... the gardens. The paths. The fountains."

"Good. Now rise higher. Above the palace. What do you see?"

"The whole palace complex. The courtyards. People walking—they look small."

4 of 10
Chapter 5

Rising Higher

"Higher still," Marcus continued. "Above the city of Rome itself. Above the seven hills. What do you see now?"

Gaius kept his eyes closed, picturing it. "Rome looks... smaller. I can see the river. The roads leading out into the countryside. Fields. Mountains in the distance."

"Now rise even higher. Above Italia. Above the sea. You can see the whole empire now—Gaul, Hispania, Africa, the Eastern provinces. Millions of people, each with their own troubles, their own triumphs, their own moments of shame."

Gaius's breath slowed. From up there, he couldn't see the festival. Couldn't see the stage. Couldn't see himself standing frozen, mouth open.

"From up there," Marcus said quietly, "what becomes of your humiliation?"

Gaius opened his eyes slowly. "I can't even see it. It's just... a tiny moment. In one tiny place."

Chapter 6

The Emperor's Secret

Gaius looked at the old man with new eyes. "Do you really do this? When you're upset?"

"Often," Marcus said. "Ruling an empire brings many opportunities for embarrassment, you know. For failure. For decisions that hurt people, no matter how carefully I try."

Gaius froze. "Ruling an... You're—you're the Emperor?"

Marcus smiled gently. "I am. Though I find the title sits more comfortably when I remember how small I am, seen from above. Even emperors are tiny specks, Gaius. Even our greatest triumphs and worst failures are brief flickers in the vastness of time."

Gaius stared. The Emperor of Rome, dressed in simple robes, walking alone in the gardens. Talking to him about feeling small.

"I worry about what people think too," Marcus said. "More than I should. But this exercise—this view from above—helps me remember what's true."

Chapter 7

The Teaching

They sat together on a stone bench. The first stars were beginning to appear in the darkening sky.

"I'm not saying your embarrassment doesn't hurt," Marcus said. "It does. Those feelings are real, and I won't pretend otherwise."

He paused, looking up at the stars.

"But that moment—that one moment of forgetting your words—is not the whole of you. It is not the whole of your life. It is not even a large part of this vast world we live in."

Gaius followed his gaze upward. The sky was filling with stars now, countless points of light.

"Tomorrow, the sun will rise," Marcus continued. "In a week, some people will have forgotten. In a year, nearly everyone. In a hundred years? No one alive will remember this festival at all."

Gaius felt something loosen in his chest. The laughter still echoed in his memory, but it seemed quieter now. Smaller.

Chapter 8

The Return

Gaius stood up. He felt different. Not happy, exactly—the sting was still there. But lighter. Like he could breathe again.

"Thank you," he said. "I... I think I understand. My troubles are real. But they're not everything."

Marcus rose and placed a hand briefly on Gaius's shoulder.

"Thank yourself," he said. "You did the rising. I just pointed the way."

Gaius looked toward the distant lights of the festival. The music was still playing. People were still celebrating. Life was going on.

"Go back, if you wish," Marcus said. "Or don't. The choice is yours. But remember: you have the power to rise above at any moment. No one can take that from you."

Gaius took a deep breath, squared his shoulders, and began walking back toward the lights. The laughter might come again. But he knew now: he could always rise above it.
